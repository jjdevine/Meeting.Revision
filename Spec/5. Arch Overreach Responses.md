## Responses for architecture overreach

Use this structure every time:

> “I’m not against it. I want the requirement that makes it cheaper or safer than the simpler option.”

Then do three things:

1. **Name the overreach**
2. **Ask for the forcing condition**
3. **Pull the discussion back to scope, ownership, and reversibility**

---

## 1) “We should use event sourcing”

### Say this

- “What exact requirement needs full event reconstruction rather than durable messaging plus audit history?”
- “If replay is not a first-class requirement, event sourcing is usually ceremony.”
- “Immutable business events make sense. Making the whole write model event-sourced is a separate decision.”

### What you are testing

- Do they need **replay**, **temporal reconstruction**, **event-first aggregates**, or just **history**?
- Do they understand the cost of versioning events forever?
- Are they ready for eventual consistency in user-facing workflows?

### Default position

For HR/payroll/talent:

- publish business events
- keep audit history
- use queues/topics properly
- do **not** jump to full event sourcing unless the domain genuinely benefits from replay and event-native modeling

### Sharp line

> “I’m fine with immutable events. I’m not volunteering us for event-sourced write models without a replay requirement.”

### Watch for

- people using “event sourcing” when they mean “put events on the bus”
- no plan for event versioning or upcasting
- reporting and query pain being ignored
- payroll correctness being exposed to fancy architecture

---

## 2) “We need a canonical model”

### Say this

- “Canonical for which bounded context?”
- “A universal model usually becomes a compromise that satisfies nobody.”
- “Shared vocabulary is good. A single universal schema across domains is usually not.”

### What you are testing

- Are they solving **translation cost**, **integration sprawl**, or just seeking neatness?
- Are HR, payroll, talent, and identity actually using the same concepts the same way?
- Who owns the canonical model when meanings diverge?

### Default position

Prefer:

- domain-owned models
- explicit contracts at boundaries
- translation where needed
- a shared glossary, not a universal enterprise object model

### Sharp line

> “I want common language, not a pretend universal truth model that every team fights over.”

### Watch for

- “Employee” meaning five different things across domains
- endless schema committees
- one central model becoming a delivery bottleneck
- lowest-common-denominator APIs

---

## 3) “Let’s use one workflow engine for everything”

### Say this

- “One engine for which class of problem: approvals, integrations, or domain sagas?”
- “A single tool for every process usually means at least one team is using the wrong tool.”
- “Standardising on one engine is attractive until it starts owning domain logic it shouldn’t.”

### What you are testing

- Are they mixing:
    - connector workflows
    - human approvals
    - long-running code-owned orchestration
    - BPM-style case management
- Is the push for one engine operational convenience or actual fit?

### Default position

Use the right pattern for the problem:

- integration-heavy -> workflow/integration platform
- domain-owned long-running process -> code orchestration
- simple synchronous flow -> keep it in the service

### Sharp line

> “I support one governance model. I do not support one engine pretending all process types are the same.”

### Watch for

- visual flows hiding business logic
- platform team becoming the owner of every business process
- forced standardisation that slows teams down

---

## 4) “We should standardize this now”

### Say this

- “What problem are we solving today that requires standardisation before we have usage patterns?”
- “Premature standardisation locks in guesses.”
- “Two or three real implementations usually teach us more than one abstract standard.”

### What you are testing

- Is the need:
    - actual repeated pain
    - platform control
    - or just discomfort with ambiguity?
- Do you already have enough examples to standardise intelligently?

### Default position

Standardise **after** you have:

- at least a few real use cases
- evidence of repeated pain
- a clear support/ownership model

Standardise the **guardrails** early. Standardise the **abstraction** later.

### Sharp line

> “I’m happy to standardise once we know what repeats. I don’t want to standardise our guesses.”

### Watch for

- architecture committees inventing frameworks no team asked for
- standards with no adoption path
- platform artefacts that outlive the problem they were created for

---

## 5) “Make it generic”

### Say this

- “Generic for which second use case that already exists?”
- “Generic usually means less clear, less safe, and slower to deliver.”
- “Let’s not generalise from a single example.”

### What you are testing

- Is there an actual near-term reuse case?
- Are the use cases structurally similar, or just superficially similar?
- What complexity gets added to support the hypothetical future case?

### Default position

Build for:

- the current bounded context
- one likely adjacent use case at most
- extension points only where there is a clear reason

### Sharp line

> “I’ll generalise when the second consumer is real, not imagined.”

### Watch for

- configuration explosion
- unclear ownership because “it’s generic now”
- weak domain fit
- generic services becoming shared messes

---

## 6) “We need to future-proof it”

### Say this

- “Which future are we protecting against?”
- “Future-proofing without a named risk usually means overengineering.”
- “I’d rather design for change than for fantasy.”

### What you are testing

- Is the future risk:
    - scale
    - regulation
    - vendor swap
    - org structure change
    - product expansion
- How likely is it, and what is the cost now versus later?

### Default position

Do this instead of vague future-proofing:

- isolate hard-to-reverse decisions
- keep contracts clear
- avoid unnecessary coupling
- choose replaceable components where swaps are plausible
- leave the rest simple

### Sharp line

> “Let’s protect the irreversible decisions, not overbuild every reversible one.”

### Watch for

- speculative flexibility everywhere
- complexity added with no owner
- “future-proof” being used to win an argument without evidence

## Fast counters that sound controlled, not defensive

Use these when you need something short:

- “What requirement makes the heavier option the cheaper option?”
- “What are we protecting, exactly?”
- “What is the irreversible decision here?”
- “Is this solving a real second use case or a hypothetical one?”
- “What breaks if we keep it domain-specific for now?”
- “I want the operational model, not just the pattern name.”
- “Who owns it after launch?”
- “What is the rollback plan if this abstraction is wrong?”

## Best responses for your domain

### Payroll

- “If replay, effective dating, and audit are the drivers, say that directly. Otherwise we may be dressing up ordinary logic as advanced architecture.”
- “Payroll needs correctness more than pattern purity.”

### HR / absence / onboarding

- “We should not centralise policy or process just because several teams use the word workflow.”
- “Shared tooling is fine. Shared business logic is not automatically fine.”

### Talent

- “Candidate, employee, and payroll worker are related concepts, not interchangeable enterprise objects.”
- “A canonical model here will likely blur real domain differences.”

---

## The strongest close for any of these

Pick one:

- “I’m not blocking the heavy option. I’m asking for the requirement that forces it.”
- “Let’s make the simplest decision that is still hard to regret.”
- “I’d rather delay the abstraction than lock in the wrong one.”
- “We can always add weight later. Removing it is harder.”

## The single best line in this pack

> “What exact problem are we solving that the simpler option does not solve?”

That line kills a lot of nonsense without sounding political.
