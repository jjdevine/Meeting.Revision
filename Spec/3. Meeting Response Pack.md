## Meeting response pack

Memorize the pattern, not every line:

1. **Classify the problem**
    “This is mainly a **rules / workflow / integration / reporting** decision.”
2. **Force decision criteria**
    “The choice depends on **ownership, volatility, audit, latency, and reuse**.”
3. **Set the default**
    “Default is the **simple option** unless a clear requirement forces the heavier one.”
4. **Stop premature product talk**
    “Let’s lock the capability first, then compare products.”

---

## 1) When someone says “we need a rules engine”

- “Before we call it a rules engine, what actually needs to change without a code release?”
- “Who owns rule changes, and how are they versioned, tested, and audited?”
- “This may just be service-owned policy plus configuration, not a full rules platform.”
- “Let’s separate config, decision tables, and a real rules engine. Those are not the same thing.”

---

## 2) When someone says “this needs workflow/orchestration”

- “Is this mostly business policy, or mostly process coordination across systems?”
- “Are we dealing with approvals and connectors, or code-owned long-running state?”
- “A multi-step process does not automatically justify orchestration.”
- “Let’s separate domain logic from the process wrapper around it.”

---

## 3) When someone says “let’s make it event-driven” or “use event sourcing”

- “Do we need a command, a business event, or a stream? Those are different problems.”
- “What specific pain are we solving that a queue plus audit trail does not solve?”
- “Do we need full temporal reconstruction, or just durable messaging and history?”
- “Event sourcing is only worth it when replay and event-first modelling are real requirements, not because it sounds clean.”

---

## 4) When someone says “put APIM in front of everything”

- “Is this an external API governance problem or just internal service-to-service traffic?”
- “Where do we actually need throttling, productisation, policy enforcement, or consumer isolation?”
- “Putting a gateway everywhere adds control, but it also adds latency, cost, and another failure point.”
- “Let’s use an API edge where there is an edge.”

---

## 5) When someone says “send it to the data lake” or “report off the platform”

- “Is this operational reporting or cross-domain analytics?”
- “A data lake is not a substitute for service ownership and clean boundaries.”
- “We should not solve integration by dumping everything into analytics storage.”
- “Before picking the storage target, let’s define the business metrics and ownership model.”

---

## 6) When someone says “split this into another microservice”

- “What bounded context are we protecting by splitting it?”
- “If the data and change cadence are shared, the split may just create chatty coupling.”
- “A separate deployable is not automatically a better boundary.”
- “Let’s optimise for ownership and change isolation, not service count.”

---

## 7) When someone jumps straight to a product or vendor

- “What requirement eliminates the simple option?”
- “Which non-functional requirement is actually driving this product choice?”
- “What is the operational model after go-live, and who owns it?”
- “Let’s agree the capability, constraints, and decision criteria before naming vendors.”

---

## Payroll-specific lines

These are worth memorising because they sound senior and are true:

- “For payroll, correctness and replay matter more than architectural elegance.”
- “Any dynamic rules story needs versioning, testability, and audit by effective date.”
- “A shared platform decision is only justified if multiple domains truly need the same capability.”
- “For payroll and HR, business ownership of change is a stronger signal than technical preference.”

---

## Lines that buy time without sounding vague

Use these when you need 20 seconds to think:

- “I think we’re mixing the business requirement with the implementation pattern.”
- “There’s a simple version and a heavyweight version of this problem. We should decide which one we actually have.”
- “I’d keep the default close to the owning service unless reuse and governance justify centralising it.”
- “This feels like a capabilities discussion before it becomes a tooling discussion.”
- “Let’s identify the decision that is hard to reverse and treat that separately.”
- “I don’t think the product choice is the first decision here.”

---

## Best delivery format in a meeting

Use this exact structure:

> “I see this as a **[problem type]** decision.
> The choice depends on **[3 criteria]**.
> My default would be **[simple option]** unless we know **[forcing condition]**.”

Example:

> “I see this as a decisioning problem. The choice depends on rule volatility, business ownership, and auditability. My default would be service-owned logic plus config unless we know rules need business authoring and versioned replay.”

That is the sweet spot: **prepared, useful, not bluffing**.

## What to memorize first

Start with these 8:

- “What requirement eliminates the simple option?”
- “Who owns change?”
- “How is it versioned and audited?”
- “Is this a capability problem or a product conversation too early?”
- “What bounded context are we protecting?”
- “Do we need a command, an event, or a stream?”
- “Is this operational reporting or cross-domain analytics?”
- “Default is the simple option unless a clear requirement forces the heavier one.”

Use those well and you will sound more prepared than most people in the room.
